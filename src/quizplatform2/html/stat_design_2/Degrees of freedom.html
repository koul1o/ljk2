Degrees of freedom

Introduction

In statistics, the number of degrees of freedom is the number of values in the final calculation of a statistic that are free to vary.

Estimates of statistical parameters can be based upon different amounts of information or data. The number of independent pieces of information that go into the estimate of a parameter are called the degrees of freedom. In general, the degrees of freedom of an estimate of a parameter are equal to the number of independent scores that go into the estimate minus the number of parameters used as intermediate steps in the estimation of the parameter itself.

The number of remaining degrees of freedom is important to determine how easy it is for a model to fit the data. If the sample size is small (limited number of observations), and if the number of parameters is high (in the model), the model will not explain much.

Technical details

The term is most often used in the context of linear models (linear regression, analysis of variance), where certain random vectors are constrained to lie in linear subspaces, and the number of degrees of freedom is the dimension of the subspace. The degrees of freedom are also commonly associated with the squared lengths (or "sum of squares" of the coordinates) of such vectors, the residuals of the models, and the parameters of t and F distributions that arise in associated statistical testing problems. While introductory textbooks may introduce degrees of freedom as distribution parameters or through hypothesis testing, it is the underlying geometry that defines degrees of freedom, and is critical to a proper understanding of the concept. Walker (1940) has stated this succinctly as "the number of observations minus the number of necessary relations among these observations."

Geometrically, the degrees of freedom can be interpreted as the dimension of certain vector subspaces. As a starting point, suppose that we have a sample of independent normally distributed observations, that we represent as a n-dimensional random vector (X1, ..., Xn). Since this random vector can lie anywhere in n-dimensional space, as long as there is no constraint on the values of each component, it has n degrees of freedom.

Now, let Xm be the sample mean (i.e. the mean of X1, ..., Xn). The random vector can be decomposed as the sum of the sample mean plus a vector of residuals: Xi = Xm + ei. The first component on the right-hand side does not depend on i and therefore has 1 degree of freedom. Putting together the second components ei forms a vector of residuals. This vector has n components, but is constrained by the fact that the sum of all residuals will be equal to 0 (by the definition of the mean). The first n-1 components of this vector can be anything, but once you know them, the nth component can be computed from the mean. Therefore, the vector of residuals in this case has n-1 degrees of freedom.

Avoid confusion and errors

Be aware that the n-1 degrees of freedom for the residuals is a special case. An example which is only slightly less simple is that of the estimation of b0 and b1 in a simple linear regression model of the type: Yi = b0 + b1 Xi + ei.

Xi is given, as the value of an independent variable, but ei and hence Yi are random, with ei = Yi - (b0 + b1 Xi). The mean of the residuals is always constrained to be 0, but the independent variable adds another constraint. Thus, there remain n-2 degrees of freedom for the residuals.

This can be generalized for a higher number of predictors (here p = 1), leading to n-p-1 degrees of freedom for the residuals.
